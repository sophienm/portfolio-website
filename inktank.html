UNDERSTAND

THE CHALLENGE

Create a web application that provides inspiration for tattoo designs while helping people make informed decisions they won‘t regret. How can I help people find their future tattoo they will be proud of? What do they need to know? And how can I help match people with a tattoo artist that fits their style and personality?

POSSIBLE PROBLEMS

  • People are often not informed enough about what getting a tattoo implies, e.g. pain, aftercare, etc. Pain is a factor that can often lead to unfinished tattoos.
  • Finding the right artist in a specific location can be difficult.
  • For some people it is not as simple as just getting a tattoo. They want to establish a connection with the artist to make sure they can trust them.
  • People want advice and not just an artist to simply execute a tattoo.

POTENTIAL SOLUTIONS

  • Allow people to search for artists/studios by style and location.
  • Create a messenger feature that will allow people to communicate with an artist directly and help establish trust.
  • Provide information all around getting a tattoo in form of articles/instructions so people know what they are doing.

OBSERVE

To gain insight on the subject I conducted a competitor analysis, a survey and multiple interviews.

RESEARCH GOALS

  • Determining how users find tattoo inspiration and artists.
  • Documenting pain points users have finding inspiration and artists.
  • Understanding how much importance the user gives to research before getting a tattoo.

By creating an affinity map I was able to organise and narrow down my users’ goals and frustrations.

AFFINITY MAP

It was surprising to me that none of my interviewees really had issues finding inspiration for tattoos, but that their main pain points were finding the right artist and enough information to feel comfortable with their choices.

The search flow, as well as sign up flow benefitted most from these changes and evolved into a smoother experience.

HIGH-FIDELITY

TEST

After having finalised the first version of the high-fidelity prototype it was time to see if my ideas worked. This involved creating a test plan, writing a test script and recruiting participants to test the prototype.

GOALS

The goal was to test the usability of the web app by giving first time users basic tasks to fulfill. The results helped asses if the users understood how to complete the tasks and if they value the utility of the web app. Based on the knowledge gained I was able to recognise weaknesses that needed to be corrected.

TEST OBJECTIVES

  1. Find out if participants can easily locate the sign up function and is the sign up process understandable.
  2. Observe how quickly the participants can find information about aftercare.
  3. Examine if the participants have any difficulties to find an artist with the provided criteria.
  4. Measure how quickly participants are able to save the artist to a newly created board.
  5. Examine if the participants understand how to message an artist and to find messages.

METHODOLOGY

Moderated in-person and moderated remote usability tests, all of which were recorded. One half of the group of participants was given the desktop version to test first and the other half tested the mobile version first.

After completing the usability tests it was time to make sense of all of the information I had collected. The first step was to create an affinity map to help me structure my findings. So many Post-its!!! I could then evaluate the severity of the issues I had discovered by entering them into a rainbow spreadsheet and rating them using the Jakob Nielsen‘s rating scale, as well adding up how many participants had the same issues.

AFFINITY MAP

RAINBOW SPREADSHEET

User testing helped me find the less obvious, sneakier issues that I was too close to see. After analysing the spreadsheet I decided to prioritise the first 5 errors based on their impact and the time needed to correct them. It only took small changes to remove these issues, but the positive impact on the user experience was immense. Most surprising to me was that all volunteers had problems understanding my sign up/log in icon (lock icon). How did I not see this before? This step showed me very clearly how important it is to get a fresh set of eyes to look at my work.

WHAT I LEARNT

Two major things I learnt are that it's absolutely ok to be wrong and that if you listen closely and ask the right questions your users will very often give you the solution. Having your users disagree with your design choices during testing is a great opportunity to understand them better and learn. It is impossible to have thought everything through and often you need a fresh set of eyes to point out the obvious.

IMPROVEMENTS

  1. I would like to flesh out the filter options by doing more research on what users look for in tattoos. This can be easily done by creating a survey.
  2. I would like to improve the main menu content. During my usability testing some users expressed that the organisation of its content and sub-content wasn’t completely obvious. By creating a card sort I should be able to learn how to iterate the menu structure.
  3. I seems that two menus (main menu + profile menu) are maybe too many and contain too much hidden information. I would like to try and integrate the content of the main menu in to the navigation tab as a horizontally scrollable menu. This would minimise the steps the user needs to take when changing categories. No need to open the menu every time and no more guessing what is hiding behind the hamburger menu. A/B testing and usability testing will help me validate my hypothesis.
